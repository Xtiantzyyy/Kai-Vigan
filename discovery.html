HISTORY

In pre-colonial times, Chinese used Vigan as a coastal trading post. Chinese traders sailed from West Philippine Sea to Isla de Vigan (Island of Vigan) by the Mestizo River. In the late-1500s, Spain colonized the Philippines and the historic town of Vigan was established. It resulted to unique architectural design which developed the awesome townscape and culture of the place. Vigan is also known as the well-preserved city and a known UNESCO World Heritage site.

It was believed that when Juan de Salcedo asked a native what the name of the place is, (as Salcedo spoke in Spanish), the native could not understand a word he was saying. Salcedo however was pointing on the ground and the native thought that he was actually asking for the name of the vegetation planted on the ground. He thereupon said “bigaa apo, bigaa apo”, referring to a tuber plant from the “gabi” or taro family. From then on the Spaniards named the place Bigan.

GEOGRAPHY

Vigan is located on the western coast of Northern Luzon. It is the capital city of Ilocos Sur. It is a big city that has an area of 28.86 km2. Vigan can be found at 120 23’ 15’’ east longitude and 17 34’ 30’’ north latitude. It is in the northern part of Luzon, one of the Philippines’ main islands. It serves as the capital of Ilocos Sur and has been a major political and trading center since before the Spanish colonizers arrived in the Philippines. Its favorable geographical features and location continue to make it a very accessible center.

It is only 408 kilometers away from Manila, 80 kilometers away from Laoag, and 139 kilometers away from La Union’s San Fernando. Agriculture is their primary industry and their major products are rice, vegetables, poultry, woven clothes, ceramics, and processed meat like Longganisa. Bigueños use Ilocano, English, and Tagalog as their language so there will be no problems you will encounter if you will visit it.

CULTURE
&  TRADITION
Longganisa Festival

Celebrated every January 22-25, it proudly features the Vigan’s product known as Vigan longganisa. During the festival, the streets will be filled with people joyfully performing different dances while others are showcasing their unique longganisa recipe. Locals and foreigners have a chance to try their cuisines. At the end of the day, the people will choose the best performers and the best cooks that participated in the festival. The festival has turned into a group cabinet as Vigan longganisa, a local rendition of Mexican salami, has a particular garlicky taste that has turned into a most loved of the Filipino sense of taste. The city has a longganisa-themed march and at one time endeavored to set a record for having the longest longganisa at any point made.

Abel Weaving

The abel is a woven product made by Bigueños. Abel cloth is made from yarns of cottons and sagut (a natural dye from black pulm). It is strong, color-ful, and has a great texture that some inherits it to their child. It is a hard process to create an abel cloth since the weavers must synchronize their hands and feet while weaving. It can be used as a blanket, sweaters, tablemat, and pillow-case.

Palarong Ramada

Each ramada is made for the feast of Sto. Cristo Milagroso de Vigan or locally called as Apo Lakay. Ramadas are made up of bamboos, coconut leaves, and light materials that forms just like a roof or shade. Fruits hanging under every ramada signifies prosperity and form of gratefulness to God’s grace and thanksgiving. They are scattered around the city of Vigan and traditional games happen under it.

Burnay Pottery

Burnay is made up of a grade A clay that can be located at the Western area of Vigan. Pottery wheel and kiln are also used to make burnay, however, a skillful person that masters the art of pottery is the most important on crafting burnay. Earlier, people use it for drinking and as a container of salt, water, and sugar. Foreigners and local visitors, nowadays, buy it as a souvenir or decoration for their garden.

Tres De Mayo

Tres de Mayo (3rd of May) is a celebration commemorating Santo Cristo Milagroso, also known as Apo Lakay. The day starts with a mass at Simbaan a Bassit, an Ilocano term meaning small church. At the afternoon, the streets are full with lion dance troupes and Kalesa parade. The ramadas are the center-of-attraction for each barangay and kids are all invited to play different Filipino games.

Raniag Twilight Festival

'Raniag' is an Ilocano word which means means brightens, to shine, light or glare. The Vigan streets are filled with lights or in Ilocano "raniag." As far as a City Government of Vigan is concerned, however, the word may be stretched to mean “twilight,” as in Raniag the Vigan Twilight Festival 2017. This was because twilight may be considered the witching hour, November being the Halloween season. So the recent Raniag festival in the Heritage City had its share of ghostly figures and horror masks. There are the lights that combat the fear of the younger about ghost stories and urban legend. The float parade is in fact the highlight of the festival featuring the creativity of the Bigueños.

Binatbatan Festival

The Binatbatan festival features a street dancing and showdown competition wherein participants use “abel” (woven cloth) costumes and sway in their original panagbatbat dances. Abel products are showcased during the festival. The street dancing depicted the long standing way the Ilocanos beat the cotton pods with two bamboo sticks to separate the seeds from the fluff called batbat. “Batbat” is the first process in making the Iluko, “Abel.” Binatbatan became another Ilocano folk dance or an occupational dance. The street dancing honors the Abel Iloko, a traditional craft of Vigan that has sustained its economy from the Pre-Spanish Period to the Present. The Binatbatan festival highlights the annual Viva Vigan Festival of Arts. The celebration of this festival has the main objective of promoting and preserving the livelihood or industry of the local fabric.

Vigan Bagnet

Vigan Bagnet is the Ilocano version of lechon kawali. Large chunks of pork leimpo, belly or pork sides, deep fried for at least two times until crispy. It is serve with tamatis and lasuna with bagoong nga munamon. Bagnet is deep-fried pork belly, fried till the skin crackles. It is eaten with rice as viand or eaten when drinking basi (wine fermented from sugarcane juice), beer or gin. It is a popular Ilocano creation, served also as topping to the popular Ilocano dish pinakbet.

Damili (Red Clay)

Made up of Bantog clay than can be easily found around the Vigan hills. Damili is the Ilocano term for molding. This terra-cotta or red-clay craft is one of the traditional industries that Vigan is known for. The making is passed on to every generation where each family has a role to play. They work together to gather materials, molding, firing, and storing their products. They can create cooking pots, stove, plant pots, and roof tiles. During 1970-1980, the Damili roof tiles were popular and in demand due to its quality and style.

Vigan Longganisa

Vigan longanisa is an Ilocano sausage delicacy with plenty of garlic and spices in the mixture. The way to describe its taste is, it is spicy, salty, tangy and garlicky. Vigan longganisa is said to be an influence of the Mexican salami. The tradition of making this native has existed since the period of the Spanish galleon trade. It is distinct from other Philippine sausages because of its use of the local sugar cane vinegar and Vigan-grown garlic, which are major products of the city as well.

Vigan Empanada

Vigan empanada’s vegetable filling is made up of green papaya that is grated, toge or mung bean sprouts, monggo or mung bean and shredded carrots. Its meat filing consist of whole egg and skinless Vigan longganisa. The double special Vigan empanada has two servings of the meat filling. It is similar to a thin taco that is fried to a crisp, with vegetable and meat filling. Rice flour for making the crust. For the health buff, Vigan empanada sans the longganisa is also available.

Calesa Parade

Vigan City, famous for its old Spanish-style houses and cobblestone streets, considers sustainable transportation an important part of its heritage conservation and preservation. Calesa is a tourism attraction in the city so annually they participate in showcasing their creative designs. All were decorated with beautiful designs mainly depicting the rich culture and heritage of Vigan. A kalesa (also known as carromata, caritela or karitela) is a horse-drawn calash used in the Philippines. It was one mode of transportation introduced to the islands in the 18th century by Spanish colonizers, and was initially reserved for only nobles and high-ranking civic officials. It is commonly vividly painted and decorated. It was a primary mode of public and private transportation during the Spanish colonial era of the Philippines, though in modern times, they largely only survive as tourist attractions.

WHY YOU
SHOULD GO
TO VIGAN?

Vigan has a lot of things to offer. Bigueños make sure to preserve their culture, history, and unique architecture for you to visit. In 1999, UNESCO declared Vigan as a World Heritage site. You will be amazed by its grid street pattern and picturesque house layouts. You can visit Burgos National Museum and Palacio de Arzobispado to know their treasured history. You can also learn pottery from Pagburnayan Jar Factory and meet GAMABA artist Fidel Go. You can enjoy your life there but you should never miss the Vigan cuisine especially pakbet, empanada, longannisa, and kankanen. Vigan is an agricultural town that’s why you can eat fresh and delicious foods that are prepared by the lovely farmers and cooks. Visiting this place would be your best travel among the rest of the world. Its culture, history, and tradition will make you feel safe and happy. This could be a chance for yourself to recharge and live a peaceful life. So how about we visit Vigan?
